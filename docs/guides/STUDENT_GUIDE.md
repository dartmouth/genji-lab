### What is Annotation?

[Explain the concept of annotation and why it's valuable for learning]

## Joining a Classroom

### Getting Your Classroom Code

[How students receive classroom access]

Your instructor will provide you with:
- A classroom code (e.g., "ENG101-FALL2025-ABC123"), OR
- A classroom join link (URL)

**Where to find it:**
- Course syllabus
- Course LMS (Canvas, Blackboard, etc.)
- Email from instructor
- Course website

### Joining with a Classroom Code

[Step-by-step instructions]

1. Log in to Genji
2. Click "Join Classroom" or "+" button
3. Enter the classroom code your instructor provided
4. Click "Join"
5. Confirm you want to join this classroom
6. Success! You should now see your classroom on the dashboard

### Joining with a Link

[Alternative method]

1. Click the link your instructor provided
2. Log in to Genji (if not already logged in)
3. You'll be automatically added to the classroom
4. You should see a confirmation message

### Verifying You're in the Classroom

[How to check classroom membership]

1. Go to your dashboard or "My Classrooms"
2. You should see your classroom listed:
   - Classroom name
   - Instructor name
   - Number of documents/assignments
3. Click on the classroom to see assigned documents

### Multiple Classrooms

[If taking multiple courses using Genji]

You can join multiple classrooms! Each classroom will appear on your dashboard separately. Make sure you're working in the correct classroom for each assignment.

**Tip:** Look for the classroom name and instructor to confirm you're in the right place.

## Creating Annotations

### Your First Annotation

[Simple walkthrough for beginners]

**Quick Start:**

1. Open a document
2. Select (highlight) the text you want to annotate:
   - Click and drag your mouse over the text
   - You should see the text highlighted
3. Right-click or use the annotation menu that appears
4. Choose "Add Comment" (or annotation type)
5. A text box will appear
6. Type your observation, question, or comment
7. Click "Save" or "Submit"

Congratulations! You've created your first annotation! üéâ

### Types of Annotations

[Overview of available annotation types]

Genji supports several types of annotations:

1. **Comments** - General observations and thoughts
2. **Questions** - Things you're wondering about
3. **Replies** - Responses to classmates' annotations
4. **Tags** - Keywords or categories
5. **Links** - Connections to other documents
6. **[Other types specific to your platform]**

**For detailed information on each type, see:** [Annotations Guide](ANNOTATIONS_GUIDE.md)

### Choosing the Right Annotation Type

[When to use each type]

**Use Comments when:**
- Sharing an insight or observation
- Noting something important
- Analyzing a passage
- [Other use cases]

**Use Questions when:**
- You don't understand something
- You want to start a discussion
- You're curious about context
- [Other use cases]

**Use Tags when:**
- Categorizing themes or topics
- Finding related passages later
- Organizing your thoughts
- [Other use cases]

**Use Links when:**
- Connecting to another document
- Showing relationships between texts
- Building cross-references
- [Other use cases]

### Annotation Best Practices

[Tips for creating good annotations]

**Be Specific:**
- Reference the exact text you're discussing
- Explain your thinking clearly
- Provide evidence for your claims

**Be Constructive:**
- Ask thoughtful questions
- Build on others' ideas
- Be respectful in disagreements

**Be Engaged:**
- Don't just summarize - analyze!
- Make connections to other readings
- Relate to course concepts
- Share your genuine reactions

**Be Thoughtful:**
- Take time to read carefully first
- Consider multiple interpretations
- Acknowledge complexity
- Revise if you change your mind

[If your system supports mentioning users]

1. Type `@` followed by their username
2. Select from the dropdown
3. They'll receive a notification

   - Ask someone a direct question

   - Include someone in a discussion

## Tips for Success

### Annotation Strategies

[Proven strategies for effective annotation]

**Before You Annotate:**
1. Read the entire document first (or section)
2. Note your initial reactions
3. Review the assignment requirements
4. Consider the instructor's prompts

**While Annotating:**
1. Select meaningful passages (not too much, not too little)
2. Explain WHY you selected each passage
3. Connect to course themes and other readings
4. Ask genuine questions
5. Be willing to take intellectual risks

**After Annotating:**
1. Read classmates' annotations
2. Respond thoughtfully to at least [X] peers
3. Revisit your annotations if you learn something new
4. Reflect on the discussion

### Time Management

[How to stay on top of annotation assignments]

**Recommended Workflow:**
1. Complete initial annotations early (not last minute!)
2. Return later to respond to classmates
3. Check back for responses to your annotations
4. Engage in ongoing discussions
5. [Adjust timeline based on your assignment structure]

**Time Estimates:**
- Initial reading and annotation: [X] minutes
- Responding to classmates: [X] minutes
- Follow-up discussion: [X] minutes

### Participation Guidelines

[Meeting assignment requirements]

**Quality over Quantity:**
- Focus on thoughtful, substantive annotations
- Don't just annotate to meet a quota
- Engage deeply with a few passages rather than superficially with many

**Meeting Expectations:**
- Check your assignment rubric
- Note minimum annotation requirements
- Pay attention to due dates
- [Other requirements]

### Academic Integrity

[Expectations for original work]

**Your annotations should be:**
- Your own original thoughts and words
- Properly cited if referencing outside sources
- Honest and authentic to your reading experience

**Don't:**
- Copy annotations from classmates
- Use AI to write your annotations (unless explicitly allowed)
- Share assignment details with students in other sections
- [Other academic integrity concerns]
